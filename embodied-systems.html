Embodied System – Sensing the Digital

An interactive motion–sound system that translates body movement into real-time sound.

This project explores how bodily movement, posture, stillness, and spatial relations can be transformed into sound. Using Kinect V2, TouchDesigner, and Ableton Live, the system tracks the performer's body and maps motion data to a layered sonic environment. The result is a performative instrument that makes embodied states audible in real time.

Concept

Body movement as a sonic interface

The body is always present – breathing, sensing, carrying history. Yet in digital environments it increasingly disappears into abstraction: tracked, analysed, optimised before it is felt. This project inverts that logic. Instead of treating the body as an object to be measured, it positions the body as a sensitive actor within a digital system.

How can the body – in its vulnerable, resistant presence – become hearable through real-time processes, without being reduced to data?

Movement, stillness, posture and spatial presence shape sound in real time – not as simple triggers, but as expressive qualities that make bodily states audible. The focus lies on relational body data: how body parts move in relation to each other, rather than where they are in space.

Embodied interaction · Motion tracking and sound · Real-time performance · Relational body data

A guiding metaphor is vibration – the element connecting movement, emotion and sound. Inspired by Oskar Fischinger's idea that all things carry a vibration: body movement as physical oscillation, emotion as internal resonance, sound as its audible expression.

How can technological processes make the body feel again – or even more?

Technical Implementation

TouchDesigner Ableton Data-Flow

The technical decisions follow the conceptual approach: rather than tracking absolute positions, the system focuses on relational body data – how body parts move in relation to each other. This reflects the idea of treating the body as a self-referential system, not a point in a coordinate grid.

Three components form the pipeline: motion tracking (Kinect V2), data processing (TouchDesigner) and sound generation (Ableton Live).

1. Motion Tracking (Kinect V2 Sensor)

The system uses the Kinect V2 sensor to track the performer as a 3D skeleton. The Kinect provides real-time joint positions (X, Y, Z) for up to 25 body joints. In this step the raw skeletal data is captured continuously. Each joint delivers absolute spatial coordinates, which form the basis for all later calculations.

2. Data Processing (TouchDesigner)

TouchDesigner handles all data interpretation, transformation and motion parameter extraction. The raw joint positions from the Kinect are processed into relational parameters. Instead of using absolute coordinates, the system calculates relative body parameters. These relational measures are more intuitive and stable for sound control.

3. Sound Synthesis (Ableton Live)

Ableton Live receives the processed motion parameters and maps them to different sound layers which respond to different aspects of the performer's movement, such as heartbeat intensity, breathing noise, drone expansion, or spatial panning. Each layer is controlled by a corresponding OSC parameter.

Discussion of Results

Discussion

During the development process several insights emerged regarding the relationship between body movement, motion data, and sound interaction. The project revealed both effective strategies and technical limitations when designing embodied motion–sound systems.

Relative Body Data vs. Absolute Coordinates

One of the most important technical insights was that relational body parameters work significantly better than absolute spatial coordinates. Early prototypes relied on the absolute position of joints in space, which made the system highly dependent on camera perspective and performer position.

  • Absolute coordinates changed strongly when the performer moved in the room
  • Camera perspective affected the values
  • Tracking instability caused unpredictable jumps

Using relational measurements such as hand–hand distance, head–hand distance, or body height relative to other joints produced much more stable values. These parameters describe the body in relation to itself, which made the system more reliable and performable.

Reduction

Another important observation was that fewer parameters and sound layers improved the readability of the interaction. In early versions the system contained many mappings and sound layers, which made it difficult to perceive which movement caused which sonic change.

  • Too many simultaneous mappings blurred the interaction
  • Complex sound textures reduced perceptual clarity
  • Internal modulation inside sounds competed with movement control

Reducing the system to a smaller set of clearly defined relations significantly improved both performability and audience perception. Clear assignments between body movements and sound layers proved more effective than complex multi-parameter mappings.

Embodied Interaction and Sound Metaphors

The use of recognizable sonic metaphors such as heartbeat, breathing, and low-frequency body drones helped strengthen the connection between movement and sound. These sounds are intuitively associated with bodily processes, making the interaction easier to understand for both performer and audience.

However, the project also revealed that the system does not detect emotional states directly. Instead, it translates measurable bodily conditions such as speed, posture, expansion, and stillness into sound. The resulting soundscape should therefore be understood as an artistic interpretation of bodily expression rather than a direct measurement of emotion.

Technical Limitations of Motion Tracking

Working with motion tracking systems such as Kinect also revealed several technical limitations that affect interactive performance systems.

  • Tracking quality depends on camera perspective and body orientation
  • Small tracking errors can affect parameter stability
  • Latency influences the perceived connection between movement and sound

These issues showed that technical stability is not only a technical concern but directly influences the artistic quality and performative usability of the system.

Importance of Testing with Performers

Testing the system with the actor Riccardo Campione played a crucial role in the development process. Feedback from real movement situations revealed problems that were not visible during solo testing, particularly regarding readability, latency, and the clarity of mappings. The collaboration helped shift the project from a purely technical experiment toward a performative instrument that could be explored through embodied movement on stage.

Future Work

Technical Development

Future work could focus on improving the processing and interpretation of motion data, for instance more refined filtering, additional motion parameters, and more complex mappings between bodily movement and sound. The sound design also offers room for expansion and experimentation.

  • Improved motion data filtering
  • Additional motion parameters
  • Extended movement–sound mappings
  • Expanded sound design
  • Spatial sound concepts

Performative Development

The system is currently being further developed within a theatre project with the acting class of Prof. Lara Martelli Hisleiter at the Filmuniversity Babelsberg. In collaboration with actor Riccardo Campione, the setup will be adapted and refined for a solo stage performance exploring themes of climate change and climate migration.

  • System refinement for live performance
  • Improved reliability on stage
